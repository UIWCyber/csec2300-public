Compliance vs Security vs Privacy

  • Security: protecting systems and data. Privacy: appropriate USE of personal data. Compliance: proving you meet external rules.
  • You can be compliant and insecure, secure and non-compliant, or secure and privacy-invasive. They are three different targets.
  • Callback (L3): compliance is a floor, not a ceiling.
DefinitionPrivacy = the right and the rules governing how personal data is collected, used, and shared, distinct from whether it is technically secure.

Privacy Principles That Cut Across All of Them

  • Data minimization: collect only what you need. Purpose limitation: use it only for the stated reason.
  • Storage limitation / retention: keep it only as long as needed, then dispose of it securely.
  • Data subject rights: access, correct, and delete (the right to be forgotten).
TipLocal AI callback (L19): minimization by architecture. Data that never left your machine cannot be misused by a cloud vendor.

Classification Drives Handling

  • Classify data (public, internal, confidential, restricted); the label drives how you handle it at every stage.
  • Secure disposal matters: deleting a pointer is not erasing the data. Use crypto-shredding or wiping.
  • Callback (L5): Git history proved that "deleted" often still exists. The same is true of backups and disks.
DefinitionCrypto-shredding = destroying the encryption key so the ciphertext becomes permanently unreadable, a fast way to "erase" data at scale.

The Four Buckets

  • Four buckets: legal and ethical; legal but unethical (dark patterns); illegal but arguably ethical (some whistleblowing); illegal and unethical.
  • Professional codes: (ISC)2, ACM, IEEE. Common duties: protect the public, disclose responsibly, avoid harm.
  • Security-specific dilemmas: responsible disclosure vs full disclosure; dual-use tools (your hashcat skills, your injection skills).
DefinitionResponsible disclosure = privately reporting a vulnerability to the vendor and giving them time to fix it before going public.

Ethics Cases

  • You find a critical vulnerability in a vendor's product. What do you do, in what order, on what timeline?

Walking an Incident

  • Detect (an alert, a scan finding, a user report), then Analyze (scope, severity, is it real?).
  • Contain (isolate, where your segmentation and kill switch from L11/L23 pay off), then Eradicate (remove the cause).
  • Recover (restore from known-good: Git revert, a clean image, L4/L10), then Lessons Learned (a blameless postmortem, update controls).
Watch outCommunication and legal duties run in parallel: breach notification (GDPR's 72h), who speaks publicly, and evidence preservation. Do not wipe the evidence while containing.

Key Takeaways

  • Compliance (rules), security (protection), and privacy (appropriate use) are three distinct goals.
  • Data type plus jurisdiction picks the regime; minimization, purpose limitation, and retention cut across all of them.
  • Legal is not the same as ethical; professional codes guide the gray zones; dual-use skills carry responsibility.
  • IR lifecycle: prepare, detect, contain, eradicate, recover, learn, and preparation was this entire course.
