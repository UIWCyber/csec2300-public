Vocabulary First - Threat, Vulnerability, Risk

  • Vulnerability: a weakness that could be exploited (an unpatched service, a weak password, an exposed port)
  • Threat: a potential actor or event that could exploit that weakness
  • Risk: the chance and cost of a threat actually exploiting a vulnerability
DefinitionRisk = likelihood x impact. How likely is it to happen, and how badly does it hurt if it does?
AnalogyA cracked window is a vulnerability. A burglar walking the neighborhood is a threat. The risk is how likely that burglar finds your window and how much they can steal.

The Threat Actor Taxonomy

  • Nation-state / APT: government-funded, patient, after espionage or sabotage
  • Organized crime: profit-driven, runs ransomware and sells stolen credentials
  • Hacktivist: ideology-driven, defaces sites or leaks data to make a point
  • Insider: someone with authorized access who misuses it (on purpose or by accident)
  • Script kiddie / unskilled: low skill, uses off-the-shelf tools, still dangerous at scale
DefinitionAPT = Advanced Persistent Threat. A well-resourced attacker who gets in and quietly stays for months.

What Motivates Them (SY0-701 Language)

  • Financial gain, data theft, espionage, service disruption, blackmail or extortion
  • Political or philosophical belief, revenge, chaos, or acts of war
  • Ethical hacking: authorized testers (white-hat) who attack with permission to help you
TipThe same technique serves different motives. Phishing feeds crime, espionage, and hacktivism alike. Judge the actor by intent and resources, not by the tool.

The Insider Spectrum and Shadow IT

  • Insiders run from malicious (stealing data to sell) to negligent (careless mistakes)
  • Shadow IT is a common negligent path: using unapproved tools that dodge security review
  • Example you already do: pasting course data into a random "free AI summarizer" website
DefinitionShadow IT = hardware or software used inside an organization without IT's knowledge or approval.
Why it mattersThis is one reason the capstone runs a local AI model later. Sensitive data never leaves your own machine.

Control Categories - Who or What Enforces It

  • Technical: enforced by systems (firewalls, MFA, encryption, branch protection)
  • Managerial: governance decisions (policies, risk assessments, vendor review)
  • Operational: carried out by people and processes (awareness training, log review, guard rotations)
  • Physical: real-world barriers (locks, badges, cameras, bollards)
DefinitionA security control is any safeguard that reduces risk. Category answers "who or what puts it into effect."

Control Functions - What the Control Achieves

  • Preventive: block the bad thing before it happens (MFA, least privilege)
  • Deterrent: discourage the attacker from trying (warning banners, visible cameras)
  • Detective: notice it happened (IDS alerts, log monitoring)
  • Corrective: fix or recover after it happens (restore from backup, apply a patch)
  • Compensating: a substitute when the primary control is not possible (extra monitoring when you cannot patch yet)
  • Directive: instruct people what to do (acceptable use policy, signage)
TipOne control can serve several functions. A camera both deters and detects. Classify by its primary purpose in that context.

Why Frameworks? From Heroics to Process

  • A framework is an organized catalog of what a mature security program does
  • Without one, security is ad-hoc: whatever the last person happened to think of
  • NIST CSF 2.0: six functions (Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover)
  • CIS Controls v8: 18 prioritized controls in three Implementation Groups (IG1 is minimum hygiene)
DefinitionFramework = a shared checklist and vocabulary for security, so teams answer "are we covered?" instead of guessing.

Key Takeaways

  • Threat, vulnerability, and risk are three different words. Risk = likelihood x impact
  • Actors differ by motive, resources, and skill. Insiders and negligence dominate real breach reports
  • Every control has a category (who enforces) and a function (what it achieves)
  • Frameworks (NIST CSF, CIS) turn security from heroics into a repeatable program
