The Question Behind Every AI Tool Decision

  • When you type into a cloud AI, your prompt leaves your device and lands on the provider's servers.
  • Then what? Logging? Training on your input? Retention for how long? Reachable by subpoena?
  • Shadow AI (a callback to shadow IT): employees quietly pasting company secrets into consumer chatbots.
DefinitionShadow AI = use of AI tools that the organization has not approved or vetted, often with sensitive data, outside any policy.
NoteThe real-world pattern is enterprises banning, then carefully re-permitting AI tools as governance catches up. Several well-known source-code leaks came from staff pasting into public chatbots.

Locality vs. Residency

  • Locality: where the data physically lives and is processed right now.
  • Residency: where law or contract REQUIRES the data to stay.
  • Local AI gives the strongest locality guarantee, because the data simply never left the machine.
DefinitionData locality = the actual physical location where data is stored and processed.
DefinitionData residency = a legal or contractual requirement that data remain within a specified place (a country, a region, an approved provider).
NoteCloud contracts can offer zero-data-retention options, no-training clauses, and regional processing. These are readable, negotiable, and auditable, not magic.

Regulated Data Changes the Answer

  • FERPA protects student records (this university), HIPAA health data, GLBA financial data, GDPR EU persons.
  • Feeding regulated data to an unvetted processor can itself be the violation, and the data OWNER stays accountable (Lecture 3 roles).
  • This is why a UIW advising office cannot paste student grades into a random chatbot, and why hospitals pilot LOCAL models.
DefinitionRegulated data = data whose handling is governed by law (FERPA, HIPAA, GLBA, GDPR), often with penalties for mishandling.
Watch out"The vendor is responsible" is wrong. Under most of these laws the organization that OWNS the data remains accountable no matter who processes it.

Model Supply-Chain Threats

  • Data poisoning: malicious training or fine-tuning data plants behaviors or backdoors in the weights.
  • Tampered weights: modified files swapped in transit or in a registry. Malicious formats: pickle-based files can execute code on load.
  • License and provenance gaps: unknown training data is legal and ethical exposure.
DefinitionData poisoning = deliberately corrupting a model's training or fine-tuning data to plant hidden, attacker-chosen behavior.
Watch out"Sleeper-agent" research shows a model can be trained to behave normally until a trigger phrase appears. Standard evaluation cannot easily rule this out. Provenance matters precisely because testing is not enough.

Verifying What You Pull

  • Digests and checksums: ollama list shows digests; registries publish hashes so you can confirm the file is unaltered.
  • Prefer safetensors or gguf (data-only) over pickle formats, and pin model versions like you pin container images.
  • Read the model card: intended use, training-data notes, evaluations. It is the model's nutrition label, and its absence is a red flag.
DefinitionModel card = a document published with a model describing its intended use, training data, limitations, and evaluation results.
NoteThe industry is repeating Lecture 8: signed models and ML-BOMs (an SBOM for AI) are emerging. Same problem, same solutions, new artifact.

Reading a Digest

  • Before trusting a model, confirm exactly which bytes you pulled. One command shows the digest.
Inspect what you actually downloaded
$ ollama list
NAME ID SIZE
llama3.2:3b a80c4f17acd5 2.0 GB
  1. 1ollama list is the program plus subcommand: it lists every model already pulled to this machine.
  2. 2NAME is the model and tag you asked for; llama3.2:3b pins both the family and the size.
  3. 3ID is the content digest, a hash of the actual file. If it matches the registry's published hash, the bytes were not tampered with.
  4. 4Compare that ID against the registry before you trust a model for anything sensitive, exactly like verifying a container image.
Tipollama show llama3.2 goes further: it prints the parameters, license, and template so you can read the provenance, not just the size.

The Questions Every Org Must Answer

  • Which tools are approved? What classes of data may enter them? Is usage logged? Where is a human required to review?
  • Map it to Lecture 3's hierarchy: policy (no regulated data in unapproved AI), standard (the approved list), procedure (the request and review flow).
  • Your capstone repo carries a mini AI-use policy in SECURITY.md, written this week.
DefinitionAcceptable-use policy = a written rule stating which tools and data uses are permitted, forbidden, or require review.
AnalogyThis is the same shape as a company's "which websites and USB drives are allowed" policy, just pointed at AI tools and the data you feed them.

Key Takeaways

  • The core question is WHERE data goes and WHO can see it; local answers it by architecture.
  • Regulated data narrows the choices before anyone's preference enters the discussion.
  • Models are supply-chain artifacts: verify digests, prefer safe formats, read model cards, pin versions.
  • Governance turns all of this into enforceable policy, which your capstone now includes.
