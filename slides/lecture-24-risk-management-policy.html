The Risk Assessment Process

  • Six steps turn a vague worry into a tracked, owned decision.
  1. 1Identify assets and what they are worth.
  2. 2Identify threats and vulnerabilities against them.
  3. 3Analyze each risk as likelihood times impact.
  4. 4Prioritize so the worst risks get attention first.
  5. 5Treat each one (mitigate, transfer, avoid, or accept).
  6. 6Monitor because risk changes over time.
DefinitionRisk register = the living, owned list of known risks, each with its analysis, chosen treatment, and status.

Qualitative Analysis - the Heat Map

  • Rate likelihood and impact on a scale (Low/Med/High or 1-5) and plot each risk on a grid.

The Formulas in Words

  • AV (asset value) times EF (exposure factor, the percent lost) = SLE (single loss expectancy).
  • SLE times ARO (annual rate of occurrence) = ALE (annualized loss expectancy).
  • Worked: a laptop with AV $2,000 and EF 100% gives SLE $2,000; an ARO of 0.1 (once per ten years) gives ALE $200 per year.
DefinitionALE = the dollars you expect a risk to cost you in a typical year. It is what you compare a control's price against.

Worked Example - Justify a Control

  • Risk: ransomware. Affected data AV $500,000, EF 40%, so SLE = $200,000. ARO 0.25, so ALE = $50,000 per year.
  • Proposed control: backups plus training, costing $12,000 per year, which cuts ARO to 0.05, so the new ALE = $10,000 per year.
  • ALE dropped by $40,000 for a $12,000 spend. Justified. This is the language that gets budget approved.
TipThe decision rule: a control is justified when it cuts ALE by more than it costs. Not maximum spending, wise spending.

Residual Risk and the Register

  • Mitigate (reduce likelihood or impact) and transfer (insurance, outsourcing) do not erase risk.
  • What remains after treatment is residual risk, and someone must formally accept it within the risk appetite.
  • Risk register columns: risk, owner, likelihood, impact, treatment, residual, status.
DefinitionResidual risk = the risk left over after your treatment. Accepting it is a documented decision, not neglect.

Key Risk Metrics for Operations

  • MTTD / MTTR: mean time to detect and to respond. MTBF / MTTF: reliability. RTO / RPO: recovery time and recovery point objectives (BC/DR).
  • SLE and ALE drive the money conversation; KRIs (key risk indicators) let you watch trends.
  • These acronyms are dense Security+ points. The exit ticket and Quiz 8 drill them.
DefinitionRTO = how fast you must be back up. RPO = how much data (in time) you can afford to lose.

Policies Operationalize Risk

  • Policies encode a risk decision as an enforceable rule: "we accept X by requiring control Y" becomes written.
  • The governance cascade (L3) still applies: policy, then standard, then procedure, then guideline, now driven by risk data.
  • Your capstone's SECURITY.md plus branch protection is policy becoming a technical control, end to end.
NoteThis is the payoff of the whole term. Every control you built was a risk decision someone could have written as policy.

Key Takeaways

  • Risk assessment = identify, analyze, prioritize, treat, monitor. Qualitative for speed, quantitative for dollars.
  • SLE = AV times EF; ALE = SLE times ARO. Justify a control when it cuts ALE by more than it costs.
  • Treatments are mitigate, transfer, avoid, and accept, and someone owns the residual.
  • Policies encode risk decisions into enforceable rules. Next session: privacy law, ethics, and incident response.

In-Class Activity

ALE budget defense.

  1. 1Card: an asset value, exposure factor, and current ARO for a given risk, plus two candidate controls (each with a cost and a new ARO).
  2. 2Pairs, six minutes: compute the current ALE, each control's new ALE, and the net benefit (ALE reduction minus cost). Recommend one, or "accept."
  3. 3Two pairs present their math and recommendation; the class checks the arithmetic.
  4. 4Debrief: sometimes ACCEPT is the right, defensible answer. Security is spending wisely, not spending maximally.
