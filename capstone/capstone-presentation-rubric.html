Capstone Presentation Rubric

CSEC 2300 Foundations of Cyber Security  |  Instructor: Dr. Gonzalo D Parra
The final presentation is worth 5% of your course grade (part of the 15% capstone). Teams present in posted time slots during the scheduled Capstone Presentations sessions (order posted on Canvas). Each team gets roughly ten to twelve minutes: about eight to present plus two or three for Q&A.

Hard Rules

  • Every member speaks. Delivery credit requires a speaking part from each team member.
  • Missing your slot costs 15%. Failure to present during the scheduled Capstone Presentations results in an automatic 15% deduction from the team's total capstone grade.
  • No late work. The final repo, wiki, and board are due at the posted deadline on Canvas or they score zero.
  • Demo fallback required. A recorded demo video must be in the repo. If the live demo fails, you switch to the recording and keep going; a working artifact is not penalized heavily for a failed cable.

Required Structure (five beats, in order)

  1. Architecture diagram plus a live demo of the running stack.
  2. Controls mapped to course outcomes (CO) and SY0-701 domains.
  3. One attack you defended (injection, an exposed service, and so on).
  4. Risks accepted plus the incident-response note.
  5. Lessons learned.

Scoring Criteria and Weights

Enter a SCORE (0 to that criterion's max points) for each criterion. The weighted total out of 100 computes automatically. Descriptors are shown as reference.

Presentation total 0 / 100

Architecture and working demo

25% Score / 25
ExcellentThe stack runs live. Segmentation, TLS at the edge, and containerization are visibly demonstrated, not just claimed. The architecture diagram matches what is actually running.
AdequateThe stack runs (live or via the required recording) and the diagram is accurate, but one or two hardening features are described rather than shown.
Missing or weakNo working stack shown live or recorded, the diagram contradicts the deployment, or the stack is a stock install with no evident hardening.

Security controls and mapping

30% Score / 30
ExcellentEach implemented control is named, classified (preventive, detective, or corrective), and mapped to a specific CO and SY0-701 domain. The mapping is correct and the team can defend it under questioning.
AdequateControls are listed and mostly mapped, but some classifications or domain mappings are vague, wrong, or unexplained.
Missing or weakControls are named without mapping, the mapping is copied boilerplate the team cannot explain, or few real controls exist.

Attack and defense narrative

20% Score / 20
ExcellentA real attack is shown against the stack and its mitigation is demonstrated, with honest residual risk stated. "This injection still partially works; here is our compensating control" earns full credit. Honest security reasoning outranks a polished demo.
AdequateAn attack and defense are described credibly but demonstrated only partially, or the residual risk discussion is thin.
Missing or weakNo concrete attack, a purely hypothetical scenario with no evidence, or claims of perfect security with no residual risk acknowledged.

Governance artifacts

15% Score / 15
ExcellentThe risk register, AI-use policy, and incident-response note are complete in the wiki, and the Git history and Project board show steady, distributed work all semester.
AdequateArtifacts exist but one is thin or stale, or the board and commit history show bursts rather than steady collaboration.
Missing or weakOne or more artifacts absent, an empty or stale Project board, or commit history concentrated in a single member.

Delivery and teamwork

10% Score / 10
ExcellentEvery member speaks with a substantive part. The talk is clear, hits the five beats in order, lands on time, and the team answers questions directly.
AdequateEvery member speaks but parts are uneven, timing slips, or answers wander.
