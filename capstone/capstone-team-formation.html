Capstone Team Formation Guide

CSEC 2300 Foundations of Cyber Security  |  Instructor: Dr. Gonzalo D Parra

The Rule

Teams are 2 to 3 students. No solo capstones, no exceptions, and no teams of four. You will build, harden, and document your secure local-AI stack with the same people all semester, so choose deliberately.

Compare Schedules Before You Commit

The single biggest predictor of capstone pain is a team that cannot find time to meet. Before you commit to a team, compare schedule availability with your prospective teammates: you will collaborate and learn together all semester, so make sure you share workable meeting times.

Have every prospective teammate fill in this grid, then overlay them. If you cannot find at least two shared blocks per week, keep looking. Enter each member's name, then check the blocks that member can meet.

Also compare honestly, before committing:

  • Work and family load. A teammate with a heavy outside schedule is fine if everyone knows it up front and plans around it.
  • Where you work best. On campus, remote, or mixed. Agree on your default.
  • Response expectations. Agree how fast you answer team messages (for example, within one day on weekdays).

Split Roles Fairly Across the Semester

Every member must contribute real commits all semester; the grader checks contribution spread, and the presentation rubric scores teamwork. A fair split is not "one person does Docker forever."

  • Name a starting owner per area: containers and compose; CI pipeline and repo hygiene; agent and guardrails; documentation, wiki, and board.
  • Rotate at the checkpoints. Swapping at least one area at each checkpoint means everyone touches infrastructure, security, and writing before presentation day, where every member speaks.
  • Pair on the scary parts. TLS, CI gates, and agent guardrails go faster and stick better with two people on them.
  • Track it on the board. Assign every Project board issue to a person. The board is your evidence of fair contribution, and it is graded.

If a Team Falls Apart

It happens: a teammate drops the course, goes silent, or stops contributing. The playbook (reference):

  1. Talk inside the team first. Raise it directly and early; most stalls are a schedule collision, not bad faith.
  2. Talk to the instructor early. If a direct conversation does not fix it within a week, email Dr. Gonzalo D Parra or come to office hours. Early means fixable: rebalancing work, adjusting expectations, or in rare cases restructuring teams. A message the week of presentations is too late to help you.
  3. Keep committing. Whatever happens, keep your own contributions flowing into the repo and the board. Your individual history is visible and protects you.
  4. Never carry silently. Doing a vanished teammate's work without telling anyone hides the problem, burns you out, and skews the grade evidence.
A team of 3 that loses a member becomes a legitimate team of 2 and continues. A team of 2 that loses a member must contact the instructor immediately to be placed, since solo capstones are not allowed.
